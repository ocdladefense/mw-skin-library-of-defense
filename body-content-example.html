Speedy Trial

Summary

Article I, section 12 of the Oregon constitution provides that “[n]o person shall be put in jeopardy twice for the same offence.” See also ORS 131.505 - 31.525 (offering overlapping, but distinct statutory protections).  That constitutional prohibition protects against “multiple prosecutions as well as multiple punishments,”  State v. McCoin, 190 Or. App. 532 (2003), and has commonly been understood to impose three general restrictions on the government:

After conviction, the government may not re-prosecute the defendant for the same offense (or, in some cases, events arising out of the same criminal episode);

After acquittal, the government may not re-prosecute the defendant for the same offense or its lesser included counterparts (or, in some cases, events arising out of the same criminal episode); and

The government may not impose multiple punishments for a single offense.

While simple in theory, these rules can lead to a significant degree of confusion in practice.  A helpful rule of thumb is that all three restrictions arise only where multiple proceedings are at issue. See, e.g., State v. Welch, 264 Or. 388 (1973) (“Double jeopardy does not occur unless a man is tried twice; it takes two trials to raise the issue.”  Therefore, the issue of multiplicity in the charging instrument is one of legislative intent and not of constitutional law) (citations omitted).

The following outline will present statutes, case law, articles and other resources that bear on the broader issue of double jeopardy protections.  In going through them, remember, the “[d]efendant has the burden to establish all elements of his claim of former jeopardy.”  State v. Norkeveck, 214 Or. App. 553 (2007).
